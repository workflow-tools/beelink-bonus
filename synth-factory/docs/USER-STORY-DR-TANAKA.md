# User Story: Dr. Tanaka Evaluates and Buys VilseckKI EDINET Data

> A narrative walkthrough of a prospective customer's journey from problem
> to purchase to publication, designed to stress-test the product, packaging,
> and edinetsynth R package from the buyer's perspective.

---

## The Character

**Dr. Yuki Tanaka** (田中 優希 / たなか ゆうき) is a 34-year-old senior NLP researcher at **Sakura AI Research Institute** (桜AI研究所 / さくらAIけんきゅうじょ), a mid-size R&D lab in Tokyo's Bunkyo ward that spun out of the University of Tokyo's information science department three years ago. The institute has 40 researchers and annual revenue of about ¥800M from corporate consulting and government grants.

Dr. Tanaka leads a four-person team building **financial document understanding models** — AI that can read a 有価証券報告書 (ゆうかしょうけんほうこくしょ — securities report) and extract structured data from it: company financials, risk factors, governance information. Their clients are asset management firms who want to automate the analysis of the ~4,000 annual securities reports filed on EDINET each year.

Her team uses **R for data analysis and Python for model training**. She personally prefers R for exploratory work and has published several papers using quanteda for Japanese text analysis. Her PhD thesis at Todai (東京大学 / とうきょうだいがく) used R extensively for computational linguistics.

---

## The Problem (Week 1)

Dr. Tanaka's team has a working prototype that extracts risk factors from real EDINET filings with 78% accuracy. To get to the 90% their client demands, they need more training data — specifically, they need documents with **known flaws** so the model can learn to handle real-world messiness: empty sections, boilerplate language, garbled formatting, and incomplete disclosures.

Real EDINET filings don't come pre-labeled with their flaws. Her team could manually annotate 500 documents, but that would take two researchers three months and cost roughly ¥3M in salary. She has ¥500K (~$3,500) budgeted for data acquisition instead.

She assigns her junior researcher, Kobayashi-kun (小林くん), to survey available datasets. He checks:

1. **Hugging Face** — No synthetic Japanese securities report datasets. SakanaAI's EDINET-Bench exists but is a benchmark, not a training set, and has no flaw annotations.
2. **Kaggle** — Nothing relevant for Japanese financial NLP.
3. **JDEX** (Japan Data Exchange / 日本データ取引所) — A listing catches his eye: *"Synthetic Japanese Securities Reports — 500 documents with quality-tiered error injection, flaw taxonomy from FSA enforcement patterns."* Listed by VilseckKI. Price: ¥45,000 for the full dataset, with a free 25-document sample.

Kobayashi reports back to Dr. Tanaka.

---

## Discovery (Week 2)

Dr. Tanaka looks at the JDEX listing. She's skeptical — synthetic data for Japanese financial documents is a new category. She reads the bilingual documentation (the `EDINET-DATASET-DOCUMENTATION.docx` we created) and notes several things that matter to her:

**Positives:**
- Quality tiers (perfect/near/moderate/severe) map directly to her use case — she needs both clean examples and flawed examples
- The flaw taxonomy is derived from real FSA enforcement actions, not invented categories
- 8 segments match the actual 有価証券報告書 structure her models need to parse
- Generated by a 70B model locally — no cloud API, which means the provider controls quality
- CC-BY-4.0 license — she can use it in commercial consulting work

**Concerns:**
- She's never heard of VilseckKI (it's new)
- "Synthetic" could mean anything from GPT-3.5 gibberish to carefully crafted prose
- She needs to verify the Japanese text quality herself before committing budget

She downloads the free 25-document sample.

## Evaluation (Week 2, continued)

Dr. Tanaka opens the JSONL sample in RStudio. She starts writing parsing code, then notices a note in the JDEX listing: *"R users: install the edinetsynth package for tidy data access."*

### How She Finds edinetsynth

The JDEX listing links to the edinetsynth GitHub repo. She sees it's MIT-licensed with a proper DESCRIPTION file, testthat tests, and roxygen2 documentation. She installs it:

```r
# From GitHub (pre-CRAN)
devtools::install_github("misawa-data-gk/edinetsynth")
```

### Why She Uses It Instead of Writing Her Own

She could parse JSONL with `jsonlite::stream_in()` herself. But edinetsynth gives her three things that save time:

1. **`segment_summary()`** — Instantly tells her if segment lengths match real EDINET filings (she knows from experience that 企業の概況 sections are typically 800-3,000 characters). She'd have to write this pivot + stats logic herself otherwise.

2. **`detect_flaws()`** — Automated flaw detection that she can compare against the quality tier labels. If the "severe" tier documents actually have the flaws the listing claims, the data is honest. If the "perfect" tier documents have hidden flaws, the data is garbage.

3. **`compare_tiers()`** — Shows her whether the tier distribution matches what was advertised (80/10/8/2). This is a basic honesty check.

### Her Evaluation Session: All 9 Functions in Action

Dr. Tanaka opens RStudio, creates a new project, and starts her evaluation. Here's exactly what she does and why, function by function.

**Step 1: Load the data** — `load_securities_report()`

```r
library(edinetsynth)
sample <- load_securities_report("vilseckki_sample_25.jsonl")
```

She immediately sees the startup message linking to JDEX and the GitHub repo. The function returns a tibble with 25 rows and columns for `document_id`, `quality_tier`, `error_rate`, and 8 `seg_*` columns. She runs `names(sample)` and `dim(sample)` — the data structure is instantly comprehensible. No time spent figuring out a custom JSON format. This is where the "tidy" philosophy earns its keep.

**Step 2: Structural fidelity check** — `segment_summary()`

```r
stats <- segment_summary(sample)
print(stats)
```

She knows from working with real EDINET filings that 企業の概況 (きぎょうのがいきょう — company overview) sections are typically 800-3,000 characters and 事業等のリスク (じぎょうとうのリスク — business risks) sections run 1,000-4,000. If the synthetic data is wildly outside those ranges, the LLM is hallucinating structure rather than content. She checks `mean_chars` and `sd_chars` for each segment — they look plausible. She also checks `pct_empty` — if more than 5% of any segment is empty, something is wrong with the generation pipeline. The `est_tokens` column tells her roughly how much fine-tuning compute she'll need.

**Step 3: Tier distribution verification** — `compare_tiers()`

```r
tier_result <- compare_tiers(sample)
print(tier_result)
# Check the ANOVA result
cat("F =", attr(tier_result, "tier_flaw_f"), "\n")
cat("p =", attr(tier_result, "tier_flaw_p"), "\n")
```

The listing claims 80% perfect, 10% near, 8% moderate, 2% severe. With only 25 documents the observed percentages will deviate, but the `deviation` column shows whether the deviation is within statistical noise. The ANOVA attribute is what catches her attention — it answers the question "does quality_tier actually predict flaw count?" statistically. If p < 0.05, the tiers aren't just labels; they have measurable consequence. This is the kind of evidence she can put directly into a paper's methodology section.

**Step 4: Forensic flaw detection** — `detect_flaws()`

```r
flaws <- detect_flaws(sample)
table(flaws$flaw_type, flaws$severity)

# Cross-reference: Do severe-tier docs actually have more flaws?
merged <- merge(flaws, sample[, c("document_id", "quality_tier")],
                by = "document_id")
table(merged$quality_tier)
```

This is the trust-building function. She's not looking at a metadata flag that says `has_errors: true` — she's looking at programmatic detection of actual text anomalies: empty sections, stubs, repetition, garbled characters, generation failures, boilerplate language. Each flaw comes with a `confidence` score (0-1) so she knows how certain the detection is. The `evidence` column shows her the exact text snippet, so she can visually verify.

She specifically checks the `non_jis_characters` flaw subtype. If the 70B LLM hallucinated Simplified Chinese characters instead of proper Japanese kanji, she'd reject the dataset immediately — her clients' production systems would choke on non-JIS text. Zero hits. Good sign.

**Step 5: Read actual Japanese text** — `extract_segment()`

```r
risks <- extract_segment(sample, "business_risks", with_id = FALSE)
cat(risks[1])
```

No automated tool replaces a native speaker's judgment for text quality. She reads three risk sections and checks: Does it use proper financial terminology? (事業等のリスク, 開示府令, 有形固定資産) Is the grammar natural or machine-translation-stilted? Are the risk factors specific to each fictional company, or is every company claiming the same generic "market volatility risk"?

**Step 6: Experiment with flaw injection** — `inject_anomalies()`

```r
# Take the "perfect" tier docs and inject flaws herself
perfect_docs <- sample[sample$quality_tier == "perfect", ]
test_flawed <- inject_anomalies(perfect_docs,
                                 flaw_type = "empty_section", rate = 0.3)
# Verify she can detect the injected flaws
detect_flaws(test_flawed) |> dplyr::count(flaw_subtype)
```

This is where the package shifts from "data parser" to "simulation environment." Dr. Tanaka wants to build a flaw-detection model — she needs to know what specific anomaly patterns look like in this data format. By injecting flaws into clean documents and running detect_flaws on the result, she confirms the detection pipeline works end-to-end. She also tests `"repetition"` and `"generic_language"` injection to see what those look like in practice.

This function is what convinces her to buy the *full* dataset instead of just using the free sample. The sample lets her evaluate quality, but the full dataset — with its natural distribution of real taxonomy-derived flaws — is what her training pipeline needs. She can't replicate that by manually injecting anomalies into 25 documents.

**Step 7: Get publication-ready plots** — `plot_quality_report()`

```r
plot_quality_report(sample, output_dir = "eval_plots/")
```

She opens the generated PNGs: segment length violins, tier distribution bars with the expected distribution overlaid in red X markers, flaw frequency chart, and emptiness heatmap. These go straight into her evaluation report to her team lead. She didn't have to write a single ggplot2 call.

She spends about 45 minutes total — 30 minutes on the quantitative evaluation (steps 1-4, 7) and 15 minutes reading actual Japanese text (step 5) and experimenting with injection (step 6). Without edinetsynth, this would have been a half-day exercise writing custom parsing and analysis code.

## The Purchase Decision (Week 3)

Dr. Tanaka presents her findings at the Monday standup. She shows the ANOVA result (p = 0.003 — tiers genuinely predict flaw count), the segment length distributions, and three risk sections she read manually.

> "The VilseckKI EDINET data is the best option I've found. The Japanese is natural, the flaw taxonomy maps to real FSA enforcement categories, and the quality tiers are statistically validated. At ¥45,000 for 500 documents, it's 1.5% of the cost of manual annotation and we get it immediately."

Her team lead approves. She walks the purchase request to the 経理部 (けいりぶ — accounting department), who need a 見積書 (みつもりしょ — formal price quotation) before they can release budget. JDEX generates this automatically for marketplace transactions, so the paperwork clears in two days. If JDEX hadn't automated this, the accounting department would have bounced the request back to Dr. Tanaka asking for proper documentation, adding a week of friction — in Japanese B2B procurement, even ¥45,000 purchases follow formal document flows (見積書 → 納品書 → 請求書 → 領収書). Dataset sellers who don't understand this lose sales to bureaucratic inertia.

Download link arrives after payment. She pulls the full 500-document JSONL.

**What if she decided NOT to buy?** The most likely rejection reasons:
- Japanese text quality is poor (reads like machine translation)
- Quality tiers don't match — "perfect" documents have obvious flaws
- Segment lengths are unrealistic (too short = not enough content for NLP training)
- Price too high relative to the volume
- Accounting department couldn't get proper procurement documents

The edinetsynth evaluation tools help us catch the first four issues before listing. The procurement doc question is an operational checklist item.

## Integration (Weeks 3-4)

**Step 8: Export to Hugging Face format** — `export_to_hf_dataset()`

```r
full <- load_securities_report("securities_reports_500_mixed.jsonl")
export_to_hf_dataset(full, output_dir = "hf_data/",
                      stratify_by = "quality_tier")
# → hf_data/train.jsonl (400 docs)
# → hf_data/test.jsonl  (100 docs)
# → hf_data/dataset_info.json
```

This is the function that eliminates the CSV encoding nightmare. Kobayashi-kun can now load the data in Python with one line:

```python
from datasets import load_dataset
ds = load_dataset("json", data_files={"train": "hf_data/train.jsonl",
                                       "test": "hf_data/test.jsonl"})
```

The `stratify_by = "quality_tier"` flag ensures each split has proportional representation of all four tiers — no accidentally putting all the "severe" documents in the test set.

Dr. Tanaka stays in R for her analysis work while Kobayashi-kun runs the PyTorch fine-tuning in Python. They never argue about encoding, column naming, or train/test splits because edinetsynth handled all of it.

**Result:** After two weeks of fine-tuning with the synthetic data, their risk-factor extraction accuracy goes from 78% to 86%. They still need real data augmentation to hit 90%, but the synthetic data closed half the gap at 1.5% of the cost.

## What This User Story Tests

Each stage of Dr. Tanaka's journey tests a different part of our product:

| Stage | What It Tests | Failure Mode |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| Discovery | JDEX listing quality, bilingual docs | Listing unclear → no clicks |
| Sample download | Free sample packaging | Sample too small to evaluate, or download broken |
| edinetsynth install | Package installability | Dependency issues, CRAN rejection |
| Evaluation | Data quality, tier accuracy, Japanese text | Bad Japanese, inconsistent tiers → no purchase |
| Purchase | Pricing, JDEX checkout flow | Price too high, payment friction |
| Integration | Data format, segment extraction | Non-standard format → hard to use |
| Publication | Citability, reproducibility | No DOI, package not on CRAN → hard to cite |

The simulation script (`R/edinetsynth/inst/user-stories/nlp-researcher-kk.R`) automates the evaluation stage — it runs the same checks Dr. Tanaka would run and produces a quality score. Think of it as a **pre-launch QA test** that catches data problems before customers see them, not as a tool for Dr. Tanaka herself.
