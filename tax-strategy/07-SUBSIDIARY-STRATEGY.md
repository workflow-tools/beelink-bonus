# Subsidiary Strategy — UK Ltd and Beyond

---

## General Principle

ML Upskill Agents UG operates as the single parent entity for all products across all markets. Subsidiaries are only worth creating when the administrative and tax benefits outweigh the cost of maintaining a separate legal entity. For most markets, the UG (and later GmbH) can sell directly via Stripe, collect VAT through Stripe Tax, and remit via filing partners — no local entity required.

**When does a subsidiary make sense?**

- UK revenue exceeds ~£80,000–100,000/year (VAT complexity + local credibility + tax efficiency)
- A market has regulatory requirements that favor a local entity (e.g., government procurement, data residency)
- You want to employ a local person (director, marketer) and need a payroll vehicle
- Tax structuring benefits justify the annual maintenance cost (~£1,000–3,000/year for a UK Ltd)

## UK Ltd — Primary Subsidiary Candidate

### Why a UK Ltd Eventually

WritingPAD targets UK nurse assessors. A UK Limited Company provides:

1. **Local credibility** — "WritingPAD Ltd" on invoices feels more legitimate to UK customers than a German UG
2. **Simpler UK VAT handling** — A UK-established business can handle UK VAT more naturally
3. **Employment vehicle** — If you hire a UK-based marketer or your son joins as director
4. **Banking simplification** — UK business bank account for GBP operations
5. **Potential tax advantages** — UK corporation tax is 25% (for profits over £250K) or 19% (for profits under £50K, small profits rate). Below £50K, this is lower than German combined rates

### The Bournemouth University Angle

You mentioned your son will attend Bournemouth University starting ~2027. If he could serve as a UK Ltd director:

**Visa considerations:**
- Student visa (Tier 4/Student Route) allows up to 20 hours/week of work during term, full-time during vacations
- Being a company director counts as "work" and must comply with these hour limits
- Director duties must be genuine and proportional to any salary paid
- HMRC and Home Office cross-reference — a salaried director role must be defensible

**Tax-efficient salary structuring:**
- Personal allowance (2025/26): ~£12,570/year tax-free
- A salary of £12,570/year (£1,047/month) incurs zero income tax and minimal NI contributions
- This is a deductible expense for the Ltd, reducing corporation tax
- Above £12,570, income tax kicks in at 20% (basic rate)
- Director salary must reflect actual duties performed — cannot be a pure tax avoidance vehicle

**Realistic role for a student director:**
- Managing UK social media accounts (Twitter/WeNurses, LinkedIn)
- Handling customer support emails from UK users
- Attending UK nursing education events (RCN conference, ADNE)
- Managing Google Ads campaigns for UK market
- 10–15 hours/week during term, more during vacations

**When to set this up:**
- AFTER WritingPAD UK has meaningful revenue (£3,000+/month MRR)
- BEFORE your son starts university (so the Ltd is established and trading)
- Consult a UK accountant familiar with student visa director arrangements

### UK Ltd Setup

**Formation:**
- Companies House registration: £12 online (same-day)
- Registered office address: can use a registered agent service (~£50–100/year) or a physical address
- Director: You (initially), potentially adding your son later
- Shareholder: ML Upskill Agents UG (100% parent) — this makes it a subsidiary
- Or: You personally as 100% shareholder — simpler but no corporate veil between UG and Ltd

**Ongoing costs:**
- Companies House annual confirmation statement: £13/year
- UK corporation tax return (CT600): ~£500–1,000/year via UK accountant
- UK VAT returns: handled by accountant or filing service
- Registered agent: ~£50–100/year
- UK business bank account: £0–20/month (Starling Business or Wise Business UK)
- Total annual maintenance: ~£1,000–2,000

**Structure option: UG owns the Ltd vs. You own the Ltd**

| Structure | Pros | Cons |
|-----------|------|------|
| UG → UK Ltd (subsidiary) | Clean corporate structure; intercompany pricing; consolidated reporting | Transfer pricing complexity; UG still taxed on worldwide income including Ltd dividends |
| You → UK Ltd (personal) | Simpler; no transfer pricing; Ltd dividends taxed at UK personal rates | Two separate businesses with no formal relationship; harder to move money between them efficiently |

**Recommendation:** Start with UG owning the UK Ltd. This creates a proper group structure and allows you to route UK revenue through the Ltd while the UG remains the parent. Your Steuerberater can advise on transfer pricing (the Ltd would license the WritingPAD software from the UG for a royalty fee, reducing UK profits and routing them to Germany).

## Ireland — Likely Not Needed

Ireland does not require a local entity to sell digital services. The EU OSS handles VAT. An Irish entity would only make sense if:

- You pursue B2B university contracts in Ireland that prefer invoicing from an Irish company
- Revenue becomes very large and you want to take advantage of Ireland's 12.5% corporate tax rate (but this creates transfer pricing complexity with the UG)

**Verdict:** No subsidiary needed. Operate directly from the UG via EU OSS.

## South Africa — Monitor

South Africa has specific rules about foreign companies operating domestically, but for B2C SaaS there is no requirement for a local entity. A South African subsidiary (Pty Ltd) would only make sense if:

- You need to employ a local sales/support person
- Government or institutional buyers require a local entity for procurement
- Revenue exceeds the ZAR 1M VAT threshold and local administration becomes unwieldy

**Estimated cost of SA Pty Ltd:** ~ZAR 5,000–15,000 formation + ZAR 10,000–30,000/year maintenance

**Verdict:** Not needed initially. Revisit if/when ProPrecept RSA gains institutional traction.

A Philippine subsidiary would only make sense for:

## Transfer Pricing Note (For UK Ltd)

If ML Upskill Agents UG owns the UK Ltd:

- The UG owns the WritingPAD intellectual property
- The UK Ltd licenses the software from the UG under a licensing/royalty agreement
- The royalty fee reduces UK Ltd profits (lowering UK corporation tax)
- The royalty income increases UG profits (taxed in Germany)
- HMRC and German tax authorities both scrutinize transfer pricing — the royalty must be "arm's length" (a rate a third party would pay)
- Typical software licensing royalties: 15–30% of revenue
- Your Steuerberater and UK accountant need to agree on the rate and document it formally

**Why this matters:** Properly structured, transfer pricing lets you allocate profits to the jurisdiction with the most favorable rate. With UK small profits rate at 19% and German combined rate at ~28%, there's incentive to keep profits in the UK Ltd up to £50K. Above that, the math shifts. This is exactly the kind of optimization your Steuerberater lives for.
